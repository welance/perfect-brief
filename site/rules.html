The Perfect Digital Product Brief

A good brief gives a product the right start. We made the standard open.

20% brief · 80% team
where the success of a digital product actually comes from
→ score a brief in the live console

We put together teams for a living, so we see it constantly: a product's outcome is mostly the team — but the brief is the 20% that decides whether that team starts on solid ground or spends the first month untangling what was meant. Usually that untangling happens in workshops: expensive, slow, and dependent on us being in the room.

So we're proposing something more modest and more useful: a way to check a brief for grip on reality, doability, and scalability before anyone starts — and we're publishing the whole standard openly, so it isn't ours to gatekeep. This page is the standard. The console scores against it. The rules live on GitHub, and anyone can change them.

the one invariant

An LLM reads the brief and judges each rule — pass, partial, fail, with evidence. It never sees the weights and never decides the number. Code does the maths and the publish decision, the same way every time.

That line is what makes a score you can argue with instead of a verdict from a black box. Every point traces to a rule and a source. Disagree? The rule is a file you can open a pull request against.

what you get back

Not a grade — a progress bar toward publication. A number, what's already strong, what would raise it, and a checkable source behind every line. The "what would raise it" list is literally your to-do to cross the line.

✓ Scope boundaries — you named what's out ("no native apps in v1").

score vs gate · two axes

The score says how good a brief is. A separate gate says whether it may publish at all. Keeping them apart is the point: a brilliant brief that leaks the client's name shouldn't go on a blind noticeboard, and no average should be able to paper over that.

The gate is four hard requirements that must all hold: a clear title, a stated problem, a budget at or above the €10k floor, and a fully anonymised (blind-safe) brief. Miss any and the brief is blocked, with the unmet ones named — even at a high score. Gate passed with score ≥ 85 → published; gate passed below 85 → published with reservation; gate failed → blocked. So a brief can read Directory-ready (say 92/100) and still be blocked because it names a payment provider and an email: quality is not the same as admissibility. This replaces the old severity-cap mechanism — a critical gap is now an explicit gate requirement, not a quiet penalty on the number. The line is fair because the score is reproducible (temperature 0), the breakdown shows exactly which rule or gate flips you, and you can revise and resubmit freely.

the ruleset · 14 rules

Each rule is one contestable file. criteria and the examples are all the model reads; weight steers the score in code; ★ marks a rule that is also a gate requirement. The examples are also the rule's regression tests. Numbers (R01–R14) are for easy reference in discussion — link any rule directly, e.g. rules.html#anonymised; the id is the canonical name in PRs and API verdicts.

why every line cites a source

In the AI era, fluency is free — a brief can read beautifully and say nothing. Anchoring each rule to an authoritative source pushes the score toward substance over polish. Sources are typed by authority, and pinned & human-verified in the PR, never generated by the model:

  • Standard — ISO/IEEE, W3C, OWASP, EN, AgID, GDPR. Authoritative and stable. Preferred.
  • Practice — INVEST, SMART, OKR, scope management, Designers Italia. Recognised professional practice.
  • Encyclopedic — Wikipedia. A friendly way in to name a concept; never the sole anchor for a normative claim.
who controls the bar

We run the directory and the bar — a conflict of interest. Making the bar open-source is what defuses it: a credible signal we're not tuning the metre in our own favour. Three gestures, kept separate:

Anyone proposes

Open a PR on GitHub. Zero gatekeeping on contribution — this is the part that says we're not the bouncer.

Evidence merges

A change ships when it passes the public fixture corpus in CI and gets review. Not because a curator likes it. Maintainership is earned by good PRs, not appointed.

Anyone forks

It's OSS. If the bar is ever unfair, fork it and run your own. That exit right keeps the maintainers honest better than any promise.

The fixtures are the immune system: a corpus of labelled briefs with expected scores. Try to quietly lower the bar to slip a weak brief through and you break the corpus — CI rejects you. To get around it you'd have to delete fixtures, and that's a visible, suspicious diff.

A rule we'd love a PR for

decision-owner — does the brief name a single accountable decision-maker? It's pure "80% team": a brief with no owner stalls. We left it out on purpose. Add it.
